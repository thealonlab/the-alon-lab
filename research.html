Research overview

In addition to the role cholesterol plays in membrane fluidity, cholesterol and its metabolites and precursors serves as hormones and signaling molecules. We are interested in how membrane proteins sense cholesterol and these sterol-related signaling molecules. The classical way sterol hormones are sensed in the cell is by nuclear hormone receptors – a class of soluble proteins that serve as transcription factors upon ligand binding. In addition, sterols can also be sensed by membrane receptors for a rapid response in a pathway termed the non-classical pathway

These membrane receptors are understudied and how they sense and respond to sterols is poorly understood. We focus on two families of membrane proteins deeply implicated in sterol biology, the EXPERA family and the PAQR family. The EXPERA family is a five-member family that contains the σ2 receptor and TM6SF2, two members that were shown to be important for cancer, non-alcoholic fatty liver disease, and schizophrenia. The PAQR family contains 13 members. Some members were shown to be receptors for progesterone and others were shown to be receptors for the protein hormone adiponectin. It is unclear how both the EXPERA family and the PAQR family signal and with what other proteins they interact. We use classical pharmacology, structural biology (X-ray and cryoEM), biochemistry, biophysics, and computational methods to answer fundamental questions in this field.
